    Overview

    A VLM-based parser specialized for Korean public-sector documents.

    This research started from a problem I hit at SoundMind Inc. while building B2B2G (indirect government delivery) RAG services. We could not predict what document structures the end user would feed in — but we did know the domain was Korean government documents. The goal was an SLM-based document parser that reads and structures these documents accurately, while fitting the practical infrastructure budget (limited GPU, limited cost).

    "Bigger is better" is no longer enough. The central question of current AI engineering is how efficiently you can transfer LLM intelligence into an SLM. We applied the knowledge-distillation paradigm Orca (Microsoft, 2023) introduced — training an SLM on an LLM's reasoning to make a small model match a large one — to Korean public-document parsing.

    We LoRA-fine-tuned Qwen3-VL-2B-Instruct on 2,667 Korean public documents and matched or exceeded the parsing performance of a Teacher model 15× larger (30B). In a 6-parser comparison, WigtnOCR also took #1 retrieval performance, validating the end-to-end causal chain "structured parsing → better chunk quality → better retrieval." Model weights, training data, and evaluation code are all open source.

    Research Question

    "Can we compress the parsing capability of a 30B Teacher into a 2B Student while specializing for Korean public documents — and does structured parsing actually translate into better chunking and retrieval downstream in a real RAG pipeline?"

    Technical Challenge — Why existing parsers fail

    The limit of pure OCR

    Traditional OCR like PaddleOCR recognizes text but does not understand document structure. In our evaluations it extracted 3 – 30× less text than WigtnOCR and missed most of the tables, forms, and complex layouts in Korean public documents.

    The limit of rule-based parsers

    Rule-based parsers like PyMuPDF4LLM are fast but recover almost no structure (~0%), failing to preserve hierarchical sections of laws or mixed table + diagram + text layouts.

    The limit of recent VLM parsers

    Recent VLM-based parsers like dots.ocr (RedNote) and olmOCR (AI2) are trained on English/Chinese-centric corpora and aren't optimized for Korean government documents — complex tables, forms, stamps, mixed scans, multi-column layouts.

    The practical limit of 30B models

    30B-class VLMs deliver high-quality parsing but require dual GPUs and slow inference, making production deployment hard. A 2B model can serve quickly on a single GPU and is a realistic candidate for edge deployment.

    Stages 1 – 3: Pseudo-GT generation → verification → refinement

    Pseudo-GT generation

    PDF page images go into Qwen3-VL-30B-Instruct (Teacher) and come out as structured Markdown — 10 KoGovDoc documents (3,637 pages) + 39 arXiv papers (864 pages), 4,501 pages total. Initial use of the 30B-Thinking model produced unstable outputs (think-tag contamination, token cutoff), so we switched to the Instruct model. Finding: for document transcription, instruction-tuned models are more reliable than reasoning models.

    GT quality verification

    We used Qwen3.5-122B as a Judge with a 5-point scale. Crucially, the judge was given text only, no images — not because we wanted "what differs from the source," but because we wanted to know whether the output itself is good enough to be training data. If you re-judge a VLM-generated GT with another VLM, you share the same visual-interpretation bias, creating a circular evaluation. A text-only LLM judge can independently score five dimensions of the Markdown output (structure, tables, completeness, hallucination, consistency) and breaks the generation-evaluation feedback loop. Repetition loops, text truncation, and reasoning leakage are all detectable from text alone. KoGovDoc pass rate 75.1%, arXiv 73.8%. Pages scoring under 3 were excluded from training.

    Data refinement

    • kogov_008 was 53% of the corpus (heavy bias) → controlled with max_doc_ratio=0.25
    • Found English reasoning leakage from the 30B-Thinking model contaminating GT → 20 pages dropped, 257 cleaned
    • Final dataset: train 2,667 / val 294

    Stage 4: LoRA Fine-tuning

    Base: Qwen3-VL-2B-Instruct. LoRA rank=8, alpha=32, target=all-linear — adapters on every linear layer of the language model. Vision Encoder and Aligner are frozen — pilot tests showed VLM visual perception was strong enough (Structure F1 79%) and the bottleneck was text-generation accuracy.

    Training setup

    • Hardware: 2× NVIDIA RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell (96 GB each)
    • DeepSpeed ZeRO-2; training time 31 min; final loss 0.075

    Stage 7: BC/CS chunk-quality evaluation

    Does structured parsing actually produce better chunks? We compared six parsers using the BC/CS metric from MoC (ACL 2025). Semantic chunking with BGE-M3 was the comparison anchor — the chunking method is identical across parsers, so only the input structure varies, enabling a fair comparison.

    Engineering challenges

    • Page-level chunking yielded mostly 1 chunk per page → BC/CS uncomputable → switched to document-level aggregation
    • CS is O(n²) → large docs (241 chunks, ~29k pairs) hung → fixed with MAX_CHUNKS_FOR_CS=50 uniform sampling
    • WigtnOCR extracts 3 – 30× more text than PaddleOCR — pure OCR misses the tables, forms, and complex layouts of Korean public documents

    Stage 8: Retrieval evaluation — end-to-end validation

    Strong BC/CS does not guarantee strong retrieval. Stage 8 closes the causal chain by measuring final retrieval performance: semantic chunking → BGE-M3 embeddings → FAISS, evaluated on 564 queries.

    Key findings

    • WigtnOCR is #1 on Hit@1 (0.739), Hit@5 (0.855), MRR@10 (0.788)
    • +22.7 pp Hit@1 over PaddleOCR; +2.3 pp over the 30B Teacher
    • MinerU leads BC/CS but ranks 5th in retrieval — chunk-boundary quality does not predict retrieval; text richness and structural fidelity matter more for end-to-end RAG

    Practical Findings

    • Thinking vs Instruct — for document transcription, reasoning models are unstable (think-tag pollution, token truncation); Instruct is reliable
    • LoRA rank sweet spot — at 2,667-sample scale, r=8 is optimal; r=32 regresses tables (-4.9 pp)
    • BC/CS ≠ Retrieval — BC/CS does not predict retrieval. MinerU is BC/CS #1 but retrieval #5. Text richness and structural fidelity matter more end-to-end
    • CS O(n²) fix — MAX_CHUNKS_FOR_CS=50 uniform sampling preserves representativeness while bounding compute
    • Page → document level — page-level chunking is too short for BC/CS; document-level aggregation fixes it
    • VLM extraction volume — WigtnOCR extracts 3 – 30× more text than PaddleOCR, because pure OCR misses Korean public documents' tables, forms, and complex layouts

    Model & Training

    • Student: Qwen3-VL-2B-Instruct (fine-tuning target)

    • Framework: ms-swift, LoRA, DeepSpeed ZeRO-2

    • PPL Model: Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct (BC/CS perplexity)
    • Embedding: BGE-M3 (Infinity server) — semantic chunking + retrieval embeddings
    • Vector DB: FAISS
